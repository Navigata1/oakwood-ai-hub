I. National Context — The AI Gap in American Churches

The American church is at a pivotal inflection point. AI adoption is accelerating in every sector of society — education, business, healthcare, and government — while religious institutions lag significantly behind. The data tells a clear story: church leaders want AI literacy, but almost none have built the structures to deliver it.

The Gap: 91% want AI education. Only 6% have built anything. That 85-point gap is the single largest unmet opportunity in the American church landscape today. The institutions that move first will define the conversation for a generation.

Secondary and post-secondary education is already moving: 80% of high school students now receive structured AI and responsible use education (EdWeek, March 2026). The question is no longer whether students will encounter AI — they already have. The question is whether they encounter it with or without the values-based guidance that faith communities are uniquely positioned to provide.

"Churches are being called to become centers of AI literacy in their communities."
Emerging consensus — faith-based technology leadership, 2025-2026

XI. Anticipated Questions and Responses

"What is the catch?"
No catch. IDC retains curriculum IP and the rights for expansion. The church receives the program, the community impact, and the historic recognition — at zero cost. Both parties win cleanly.
"What if students misuse AI?"
That is precisely why structured teaching matters. The 86% already using AI are doing so without supervision. We are adding the values layer — the one only a faith-based institution can authentically provide.
"Do we need to purchase equipment?"
No. All tools used in the program are free-tier or provided by IDC. Students use their own devices — phones, tablets, laptops. No procurement budget is required.
"How is this different from a coding class?"
This is not coding. This is AI literacy — the ability to use intelligence tools across every domain: writing, business, ministry, daily life, and health. Coding is one small component of a much broader skillset.
"Can this work for our community specifically?"
In our test session, a 10-year-old built a working app. A 70-year-old learned to manage health records with AI. The only prerequisite is the ability to have a conversation. This was designed for every generation of the Oakwood community.
"What is the time commitment for church staff?"
James's primary role is relationship bridge and community connection — the technical instruction is handled by Jon. Staff time commitment is minimal beyond room scheduling and communication support.
"What happens after 12 weeks?"
The program is designed to continue. Advanced tracks, deep-dives, 1-on-1 coaching, and community workshops extend the model indefinitely. The 12-week structure is the foundation, not the ceiling.
