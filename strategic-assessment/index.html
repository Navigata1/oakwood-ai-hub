Executive thesis

The technology decision is really an institutional survival decision.

The source packet is consistent: Oakwood’s immediate risk is not that Jenzabar is inadequate. The risk is misallocating scarce capital into a disruptive ERP migration while enrollment and revenue are structurally declining.

Board decision

Halt ERP migration activity now. Require enrollment stabilization, operating surplus, requirements analysis, and documented system failure before reopening the question.

Actionable alternative

Optimize current Jenzabar usage, deploy targeted managed services, negotiate vendor pricing, and redirect management attention toward enrollment recovery and retention.

Reason for urgency

At the current decline rate, the institution is projected to face sub-1,000 applicant risk within 2–3 academic years if no targeted intervention occurs.

Data interpretation

The 2021–2022 bump must be isolated before leadership draws conclusions.

What happened

CARES Act funding temporarily converted students who may not otherwise have financially cleared into enrolled students. The apparent recovery reversed once emergency funding ended.

Board implication

Do not use 2021–2022 as a benchmark for recovery, financial targets, or vendor justification. It was a policy artifact, not organic institutional momentum.

Any strategy anchored to the CARES window risks overstating Oakwood’s revenue base and understating the urgency of enrollment recovery.

Trend phases

Four phases explain the current risk better than a single trendline.

Phase I · Stable foundation · 2015–2016

Average applicants near 1,970; average enrolled near 1,748; financially cleared rate held around 88.6%.

Phase II · Onset of decline · 2017–2020

Demand softened, then COVID compounded an already-developing structural trend. By 2020, enrolled decline reached the worst single-year decline to that point.

Phase III · Artificial recovery · 2021–2022

Federal emergency relief temporarily improved financially cleared counts. The packet explicitly warns not to treat this as institutional recovery.

Phase IV · Accelerating collapse · 2023–2026

Post-CARES losses resumed with greater severity. Current analysis places decline at approximately 9–12% per academic year, worse than the previously discussed 7% figure.

The correct question is not “which ERP has more features?” It is “which decision preserves Oakwood’s ability to survive and recover?”

ERP decision gate

Oakwood does not currently meet the threshold to reopen migration.

The migration framework identifies seven triggers, 10+ sub-organization assessments, seven cultural risk factors, and 10 internal analyses that should precede vendor engagement.

Threshold not met

Source analysis says Oakwood demonstrates none of the seven migration triggers at the critical threshold level and only two to three at moderate level.

Prerequisites missing

No completed utilization audit, 5-year TCO model, process maturity assessment, data quality audit, or change readiness assessment.

Cultural risk

Small HBCU, faith-based constraints, shadow systems, and 20+ years of Jenzabar history raise the risk of change resistance and over-customization.

Future gate

Revisit only after enrollment stabilization, operating surplus, documented system failure, vendor-neutral requirements, and cultural readiness review.

IT service delivery

The practical answer is a targeted hybrid IT model.

Neither pure outsourcing nor pure in-house delivery is optimal. Keep strategic control internal; outsource commodity or highly specialized functions where vendors have a genuine capability advantage.

Keep in-house

  • IT leadership and strategy
  • Jenzabar ERP administration
  • Academic/faculty technology support
  • Vendor and contract management
  • Data governance, FERPA/GLBA oversight

Outsource first

  • Cybersecurity monitoring / MSSP
  • Tier-1 help desk
  • Backup and disaster recovery
  • Cloud infrastructure where appropriate
  • Email/productivity suite operations

Cybersecurity outsourcing is urgent: a $3.5M+ average higher-ed breach risk dwarfs the annual cost of a managed security contract.

Action roadmap

What leadership should do next

Halt ERP migration planning and vendor discussions

Stop the decision path before sunk-cost momentum builds.

Recalibrate all financial projections to 9–12% annual decline

Replace any 7% planning assumption and exclude CARES-era benchmarks.

Launch enrollment recovery and mid-year retention intervention

Retention is the fastest lower-cost lever compared with new platform migration.

Commission Jenzabar optimization/utilization audit

Most institutions use only 40–60% of available ERP features; prove the gap before buying a new system.

Issue targeted RFPs for MSSP, help desk, and backup/DR

Move quickly where outsourcing reduces risk without surrendering institutional strategy.

Establish an ERP re-evaluation gate

Require three years of enrollment stability/growth, operating surplus, documented system failure, and completed requirements/culture assessments.

Conclusion

Discipline beats visible action.

Enrollment is the lens

Every technology and capital decision must be judged against the current trajectory, not an optimistic recovery story.

Jenzabar is not the disease

The operational stress flows from declining revenue and institutional capacity pressure; ERP migration treats the symptom.

Hybrid IT is the bridge

Managed services can reduce risk and improve coverage without consuming the capital and attention required for ERP migration.

Preserve capital, stabilize enrollment, strengthen security, and defer ERP migration until the institution can afford the risk.
