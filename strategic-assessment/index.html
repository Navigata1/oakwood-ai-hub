Reading the data correctly

The 2021–2022 recovery was not organic growth

What the numbers show

Fall 2021 surged roughly 24% versus the prior year, then reversed sharply by Fall 2022. The pattern matches federal aid disbursement cycles, not sustained institutional momentum.

Strategic implication

Using the CARES spike as proof that enrollment can naturally rebound would be analytically incorrect. Federal stimulus periods must be isolated before drawing conclusions.

The CARES Act created a one-time enrollment event. It should not be used as evidence that core enrollment performance has recovered.

ERP strategy

Why migration is being proposed — and why it should not happen

01

Vendor sales pressure

ERP migration is a high-margin vendor product, especially attractive when institutions are anxious.

02

Misdiagnosed root cause

Operational stress is being attributed to the platform when the deeper cause is revenue decline.

03

Sunk cost bias

Past IT investments can create pressure to “complete the journey,” even when future spend is unjustified.

04

IT self-interest

New platforms can expand internal technical footprint and perceived indispensability.

05

Visible action bias

Leadership under pressure can prefer large initiatives over enrollment root-cause work.

06

Migration cost reality

Oakwood-scale ERP migration commonly runs $2–5M+ over 3–5 years, plus staff diversion.

Decision framework

Conditions that would justify revisiting ERP migration

None of these conditions currently exist at Oakwood.

1. Enrollment stabilization

Three or more consecutive academic years of stable or growing enrollment, independent of federal aid programs.

2. Operating surplus

A consistent surplus sufficient to absorb multi-year implementation disruption without emergency borrowing.

3. Documented system failure

A formal inability by Jenzabar One to support a revenue-critical function — not a preference.

4. Full requirements analysis

Vendor-neutral requirements documentation before RFPs or vendor demonstrations.

5. Cultural readiness

Independent assessment of staff readiness, change capacity, and implementation risk.

IT service delivery

Use a hybrid model, not a full internal buildout

Keep in-house

Strategic IT planning, vendor management, ERP administration, data governance, institutional reporting, academic relationship management, FERPA oversight.

Outsource / managed service

Cybersecurity/MSSP, help desk tier-1 operations, cloud infrastructure, backup and disaster recovery, Microsoft 365 productivity services.

Institutional knowledge and mission-critical data cannot be fully delegated. Specialized coverage, however, can often be delivered better and cheaper through vendors.

Strategic roadmap

Priority actions for leadership

Immediate

Halt ERP migration planning/vendor discussions. Reframe enrollment analysis excluding CARES. Commission enrollment strategy review focused on root causes.

Near-term · 3–6 months

Issue RFPs for MSSP and help desk managed services. Negotiate Jenzabar One pricing/support. Define any real Jenzabar gaps requiring vendor resolution.

Strategic · 6–18 months

Implement the hybrid IT model. Establish enrollment-linked capital expenditure thresholds. Re-evaluate ERP only if stabilization conditions are met.

Conclusion

The path forward requires discipline over action

Enrollment is the lens

Every technology, staffing, and capital decision must be filtered through enrollment trajectory.

Jenzabar is not the problem

The root cause of operational stress is revenue pressure from enrollment decline.

Managed services are the bridge

Preserve capital for enrollment recovery. Do not let a technology migration become the decision that compounds institutional decline.
